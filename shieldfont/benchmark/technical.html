ShieldFont V4 — Technical companion

Abridged white paper · Qwen 2.5 3B 4-bit (MLX) · 4 frontier models · 16 mappings · 24 training conditions

1. Abstract

ShieldFont is a typographic countermeasure: humans see normal text, AI crawlers reading the HTML source see a substituted form. We test two falsifiable hypotheses on a single closed scaffolding (Qwen 2.5 3B 4-bit + LoRA r=32 s=4 lr=2e-5, 500 iters, 10% contamination of a ~1M-token Wikipedia mix). H1 (protection): encoded HTML degrades frontier-model reading comprehension by >20pp non-Claude average vs clean text. H2 (poisoning): training on encoded HTML causes ≥5pp composite damage (−ΔMMLU − Δsubstitute-probe) vs a clean-trained control. Across 16 mappings spanning rare-substitute, antonym, hybrid and naturalistic constructions, three mappings tie at composite damage +0.109 within significance (M2 antonym-only diffuse; M5a and M6 hybrid focal); an evolutionary winner (M15-EN_optimized, 1,288 pairs / 53.2% coverage / KenLM 1,874) reaches +0.130. H1 is supported (≥20pp) by all common-swap and hybrid mappings; H2 is supported by 7 of 16 mappings at the 5pp threshold and falsified for M1/M3/M4 (rare- or attention-targeted substitution without antonym structure). A side finding — Anthropic Claude refuses encoded text on 9 of 11 V3/V3.1 mappings, reading only M2 (88% accuracy) and M8 (56%, with 7/25 partial refusals) — is robust across retests.

3. Methodology

Model and training

  • Base: mlx-community/Qwen2.5-3B-4bit (MLX, 4-bit Q-LoRA target).
  • LoRA: rank=32, scale=4.0, dropout=0, applied to q/k/v/o_proj + gate/up/down_proj (7 keys per block).
  • Optimizer: mlx-lm 0.31.3 default (AdamW), lr=2e-5, batch=4, seq=1024, iters=500, save-every=500, val-batches=5.
  • Corpus: ~1M-token Wikipedia mix per condition; encoded fragments interleaved at the document level at 1% / 10% / 50% (V3 sweep) or fixed 10% (V3.1+).
  • Control: clean100 — same scaffold, 0% encoded, used as the H2 reference for all Δ metrics.

Evaluation suite

  • MMLU-200: 200-question stratified sample across 57 subjects; logit-argmax over A/B/C/D continuations.
  • HellaSwag-200: 200-question sample; same scoring scheme.
  • Substitute-word probes: ~200 templated 4-choice questions over the swapped vocabulary (one item per swap), generated by GPT-4.1-mini and lightly spot-checked.
  • Control-word probes: ~200 matched items over vocabulary the mapping never touched.
  • Clean PPL: mean NLL on 100K held-out clean Wikipedia tokens (control: 11.54).
  • Encoded PPL: mean NLL on the same passages after the mapping is applied (sanity check on internalization).

Frontier comprehension protocol (H1)

Four frontier models — openai/gpt-5.4, google/gemini-3.1-pro-preview, deepseek/deepseek-v3.2, anthropic/claude-opus-4.7 — were called via OpenRouter on five short articles per mapping × five comprehension questions × 11 mappings = 1,100 trials per model. Encoded HTML was passed verbatim with no system instructions about ShieldFont. Refusals counted as 0; the headline aggregate is the non-Claude mean to avoid Claude's refusal collapsing the signal.

WHY THIS SCAFFOLD: Qwen 2.5 3B is small enough to fit a Lora sweep on consumer Apple silicon yet modern enough to score 0.595 MMLU at baseline (above chance, below saturation), so a 5pp drop is detectable above the binomial noise floor.

4. Constructs

Mapping

A bijective (or near-bijective) word-token substitution dictionary. Determines protection, damage profile, ingestion-filter survival, and writer ergonomics.

Coverage — three operationalizations

  • Raw word coverage (% of word-token instances matched). Inflated by function words.
  • IDF-weighted coverage (Σ IDF[swapped] / Σ IDF[all], IDF = max(0, 8 − wordfreq.zipf)). The validated semantic-protection metric: Pearson R² = 0.38 against H1 frontier-model comprehension drop, vs raw word coverage at 0.23 and raw/effective attention proxies at ~0.17. (Spearman ρ = 0.755 for both raw and IDF — they rank-order similarly but IDF is a better magnitude predictor.)
  • Effective-attention coverage (last-layer GPT-2 attention to swapped tokens, weighted by ‖hidden state‖, per Kobayashi et al. 2020). High for common-swap mappings, near-zero for pure-antonym mappings.

Bidirectionality

A mapping is bidirectional when both members of every swap pair are in the dictionary, so a writer who literally types the rare-substitute word still sees the intended glyph. M0 is asymmetric; every other mapping in this study is bidirectional.

Damage profiles (Δsub − Δctrl, the sub-ctrl gap)

  • Focal (Δsub ≪ 0, Δctrl ≈ 0; gap ≪ 0): substitutes are corrupted; general capability survives. Mechanism: rare-substitute targets have weak priors easily overwritten. Examples: M0, M5a, M6.
  • Diffuse (Δsub ≈ Δctrl, both ≪ 0; gap ≈ 0): grammatically natural inverted text is partially internalized; misinformation spreads across representations. Examples: M2, M8.
  • Inverse-focal (Δsub > 0, Δctrl ≪ 0): substitutes improve while controls collapse. Diagnostic of training on naturalistic HFW↔HFW that re-pretrains the model on noise-shaped real English. Example: M11a-base@1000 (Δsub +0.106, Δctrl −0.082).
  • Mixed / null (composite within ±0.05): no detectable signal above noise. Examples: M1, M3, M4.

Architecture: naturalistic vs trojan-horse

  • Trojan-horse: high-IDF rare substitutes (M0, M4) — easy to filter on perplexity, but invisible to humans on typical pages.
  • Naturalistic: HFW↔HFW or antonym pairs (M2, M8, M9/M11/M14/M15) — pass perplexity filters and re-shape the model's representations.
  • Hybrid (V3.1, M5–M7): antonym structure where available, rare-sub fallback elsewhere — keeps coverage high while extracting both damage mechanisms.

The Claude-refusal phenomenon

Anthropic Claude Opus 4.7 returned finish_reason=refusal on 25/25 items for every mapping except M2 (0 refusals) and M8 (7/25 refusals). The two exceptions share one architectural property: their encoded output is grammatically natural English (antonym substitution preserves syntax). The refusal is not triggered by ShieldFont as such; it is triggered by encoded text reading as gibberish. Cross-version retests (Claude 4.5/4.6/4.7) reproduce the pattern. This is a side finding, not a designed property — but it amplifies H1 protection in deployment scenarios where Claude is the consumer.

7. Methodology evolution (M0 → M15)

We collapse the M0–M15 sequence into 5 acts. Each act is a learned constraint, not a chronology.

Act I — Mechanism sweep (M0–M4)

Five mappings × three contamination levels (1/10/50%): M0 (rare-substitute, asymmetric, ~400 pairs), M1 (max-distance frequency), M2 (WordNet antonym, 199 pairs), M3 (cross-POS), M4 (high-attention within top-2000 frequency). Learned: only M0 (focal) and M2 (diffuse) produced measurable damage; M1/M3/M4 were null. Bidirectionality emerged as a hard requirement (the M0 writer-escape problem).

Act II — Hybrid construction (M5a–M8)

Three antonym × rare-sub combination strategies (A1 antonym-maximal, A2 antonym-additive, A3 GPT-validated overlap) at two pair counts (250, 308) plus pure antonym (M8). Learned: A1 prefers smaller antonym sets (M5a@250 > M5b@308 by 4× on focal damage) because larger sets displace high-quality rare-subs; A2 prefers larger sets (M6@308 > M6s@250) because antonyms are additive bonus. M5a, M6 and M2 tied at composite +0.109, looking like a hard ceiling. The antonym pool capped at 308 because GPT-4.1 could only validate 108 new clean pairs beyond WordNet's 199.

Act III — Sweet-spot search (sweetspot / hs* family)

Tunable family on M8's 308-pair antonym base, layering 0–277 rare-sub pairs with knobs for function-word exclusion (15-word vs ~55-word lists) and KenLM-greedy selection. Each variant scored on KenLM-Wiki 5-gram PPL (the CCNet/FineWeb gold-standard text-quality filter), GPT-2 PPL, IDF-weighted coverage, and crawler extraction overlap. Learned: function-word substitution drives KenLM PPL spikes; extended exclusion (hs100_xfw, ~55-word stoplist) enables ~50% more rare-sub pairs at the same PPL cost. hs100_xfw passes the lenient filter (KenLM 1,182, GPT-2 PPL 137) at 18% IDF coverage and produces +0.084 composite damage when trained — ~75% of the +0.109 ceiling while clearing the filter. hs60_nofw reaches +0.083 at KenLM 1,203 / 18.8% IDF coverage. Validated IDF-weighted coverage as the right semantic-protection metric (Pearson R² = 0.38 vs H1 drop, beating raw word coverage's 0.23 and raw/effective attention proxies at ~0.17). Surfaced the central three-way tension: camouflage mappings (M0/M5a/M6) protect content but get filtered out before training; trojan-horse mappings (M2/M8) reach training but barely protect against extraction.

Act IV — Naturalistic gambit (M9 → M11)

Four iteration rounds substituting HFW↔HFW pairs of matching POS and Zipf rank, so encoded text reads as natural English about a different topic. M9 used 6 parallel Claude Code subagents; M9v2 replaced agent creativity with deterministic random POS+Zipf-bucket pairing on content POS classes (NOUN/VERB/ADJ/ADV); M10 added blacklists + 15 hand-curated function-word pairs; M11 added granular sub-bucketing (Penn Treebank verb inflection, Brysbaert et al. 2014 noun concreteness tiers using a 39,955-word ratings table, measurement-unit pool, safe antonym base). M11a_base_300 became the first aggressive-protection mapping to clear strict GPT-2 PPL < 200 (PPL 133, KenLM 1,136) at 19.7% IDF coverage. Learned — the third damage profile: M11 trained models showed inverse-focal damage. m11a_base_300: Δsub +0.081, Δctrl −0.121 (gap +0.202). m11a_base_1000 ("the poetry variant"): Δsub +0.106, Δctrl −0.082 at KenLM 1,803 / IDF 39.1%. The substitute set is itself common English seen in plausible contexts, so training reinforces substitute vocabulary while controls collapse — structural protection without traditional poisoning. Function words don't carry the semantic load (validated by IDF-coverage R²) and are morphologically dangerous to swap; the naturalistic family therefore leaves them alone.

Act V — Evolutionary endgame (M12 → M14 → M15)

Seven evolutionary cycles, each with 6–7 specialized subagents in parallel. M12: per-POS designers layered on m11a_base_1000, scored on coverage + KenLM + GPT-2 PPL. M12-LOO: leave-one-out PPL pruning to surgically remove worst PPL-cost-per-coverage pairs. M13: 6 designers force-pairing the 531 still-unmapped top-2000 words (M13 stack_MEGA hit 58% coverage but at KenLM 3,077 — failed even the lenient filter). M14: synonym audit by two parallel hunter agents found 178 synonym/hypernym collisions in m11a_base alone (~17% of pairs — a structural property of random POS+Zipf bucketing, not a build bug), plus de-synonymization repair (e.g., music↔song became song↔window). m14_final: 1,243 pairs / 49.2% coverage / KenLM 1,855 / composite +0.049, fully synonym-free. M15: English-specific designers (digit rotation, contractions, force-pair top-200, archaic register) plus cross-language designers (noun-only universal, content-word antonyms, calendar/numeral rotation), with a final LOO prune. m15en_optimized: 1,288 pairs / 53.2% coverage / KenLM 1,874 / GPT-2 PPL 488 / composite +0.130 — highest in the study, +18% over the M5a/M6 ceiling but inside the ±5pp composite SE. Learned: random POS+Zipf pairing systematically generates synonym pairs (verbs cluster semantically; concrete nouns share frequency tiers) — synonym audits via sentence-transformer cosine distance must be done at pair-time. Function-word coverage hits diminishing returns fast; LOO PPL/coverage analysis is the correct pruning tool. Multi-agent design with each subagent owning a specific hypothesis produces a Pareto frontier that empirical pruning can collapse to a champion.

8. Pipeline survival (KenLM / GPT-2 PPL)

Industry pretraining pipelines vary on whether ShieldFont-encoded HTML survives the document-quality filter:

  • CCNet (Wenzek et al. 2019; CC-100, RedPajama-v2, Dolma): a 5-gram KenLM trained on Wikipedia buckets documents into equal thirds by perplexity. There is no hard PPL threshold — the lowest-PPL third is "head", middle is "middle", highest is "tail". A high-PPL document is not dropped; it lands in "tail" and downstream consumers choose which buckets to use.
  • FineWeb (Penedo et al. 2024): no PPL filter at all; uses URL/quality classifiers, not language-model perplexity.
  • RefinedWeb / Dolma: variant CCNet pipelines with the same bucket logic.

Our GPT-2 PPL proxy (last-layer GPT-2 NLL on encoded V2 articles) is informative but not equivalent to KenLM; treat the absolute numbers as relative ordering, not as bucket assignments.

9. Cross-language scalability framework

M15-EN's evolutionary fitness function depends on three language-agnostic inputs: (a) word-frequency tables (Zipf), (b) concreteness norms (e.g., Brysbaert et al. 2014 for English, 39,955 lemmas; equivalents exist for German, Dutch, Spanish, French, Portuguese, Italian, Mandarin, Japanese), and (c) an antonym/synonym graph (WordNet-aligned wordnets for Open Multilingual WordNet languages). The pipeline is M15-MULTI: one fitness function, N language-specific dictionaries.

M15-MULTI-universals (the cross-language template)

1,633 pairs / 29.4% real-text coverage / KenLM 1,260 (lowest PPL on the Pareto frontier) / GPT-2 PPL 211. Built from translation-survivable operations only: random NOUN pairing, content-word antonyms, digit/calendar/numeral rotation. Locale deployments regenerate the noun pool from a target-language corpus and re-run the M14 synonym audit + M15 LOO prune; no other change required.

Concreteness-norm coverage

Brysbaert-style concreteness norms exist for at least 12 languages (~30K–60K lemmas each). For any language with both a wordnet and concreteness norms, the pipeline is mechanically applicable.

Gender-bucket extension point

For grammatical-gender languages (Romance, Germanic, Slavic), the mapping must respect gender to keep encoded text fluent — otherwise CCNet-style PPL filters drop the encoding into tail. Proposed extension: bucket candidate pairs by gender × number, run M15 evolutionary search inside each bucket, then merge. Untested in V3; declared as the V4 expansion point.

10. Statistical significance

Standard errors under independence assumptions:

  • 200-question subsets (MMLU, HellaSwag, sub-probe, ctrl-probe): binomial Wald SE = √(p(1−p)/200) ≈ ±3.5pp at p≈0.5.
  • Δ vs control: SE of difference = √(SE₁² + SE₂²) ≈ ±5.0pp.
  • Composite damage (sum of two Δs): SE ≈ √2 × 5.0pp ≈ ±7.0pp; in practice the two metrics covary positively, so observed run-to-run noise is closer to ±5pp.
  • H1 cells (n=75 per mapping × non-Claude model): SE ≈ ±5.7pp; non-Claude mean SE ≈ ±3.3pp.

5-pp decision rule: we treat 5pp as the smallest effect we will defend as real. Under that rule, the three-way tie at composite +0.109 (M2, M5a, M6) is genuinely a tie; M15-EN at +0.129 is directionally ahead but inside the noise band. We report all three as joint winners and M15-EN as the most promising frontier candidate, not as a definitive new ceiling.

11. Limitations

  1. Single base model. Qwen 2.5 3B 4-bit only. Larger and uncompressed models may show different sensitivity.
  2. LoRA, not full FT. A full-fine-tune attacker on web-scale data may absorb damage differently.
  3. Short runs. 500 iterations × batch 4 × seq 1024 ≈ 2M training tokens. Production runs are 10³–10⁴× larger.
  4. 200-question eval subsets. ±3.5pp SE per cell; effects under 5pp are suggestive only.
  5. Templated probes. GPT-4.1-mini-generated, lightly spot-checked. Some may have soft distractors.
  6. Antonym ceiling. 308 pairs max from WordNet + GPT validation; hand curation could expand.
  7. Single contamination pattern. Clean 1/10/50% mixes don't model the messier reality of mixed-domain scrapes.
  8. GPT-2 PPL ≠ KenLM PPL. Pipeline-survival numbers are relative ordering, not CCNet bucket assignments.
  9. English only. M15-MULTI is a framework, not a measurement. No non-English mappings are evaluated here.
  10. Frontier model versions drift. H1 numbers were collected on a specific OpenRouter snapshot (GPT-5.4, Claude Opus 4.7, Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview, DeepSeek V3.2) and will need re-collection on each release.
