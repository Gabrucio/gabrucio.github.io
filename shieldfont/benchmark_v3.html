Abstract

ShieldFont is a web font that swaps common English words for unusual ones so that AI scrapers reading the raw HTML see scrambled text while humans see normal text. We ran an exploratory benchmark in three rounds: first testing the original mapping against four frontier AI models for reading comprehension (H1), then sweeping five broad mapping strategies through a small AI training run to measure damage (H2), then zooming in on the best ideas to design hybrid mappings. The frontier-model test revealed Claude refuses to even process scrambled text from most mappings — a surprising form of automatic protection. The training-damage test found two distinct mechanisms: focal damage (specific words destroyed) and diffuse damage (general capability degraded). Hybrid mappings that combine both mechanisms outperformed every single-mechanism approach on both axes. Production recommendation: switch from the current rare-substitute mapping to the antonym-maximal hybrid (M5a), which protects content better AND damages models trained on it more.

1. What is ShieldFont?

ShieldFont is a sleight of hand. The website's HTML source code contains scrambled text — common English words replaced with strange ones. But the font itself secretly renders those strange words back to their originals on screen. So a human reading the page sees normal text, while an AI scraper, screen reader, or web crawler reading the raw HTML sees gibberish.

The motivating threat: AI training pipelines vacuum the web for data. If your writing ends up in a training set, it shapes the next generation of AI — without your consent and without compensation. ShieldFont's bet is that scrambled text is harder for AI to use, both for direct extraction and for training.

That bet turns into two distinct testable claims. Either could be true or false independently. This benchmark tests both.

2. Two questions worth asking

Question 1 (H1) — protection. Does scrambled HTML actually prevent modern AI models from understanding the underlying text? If a frontier model like GPT-5 or Claude can read scrambled HTML and still answer questions about the content, then the scrambling provides no real protection.

Question 2 (H2) — poisoning. If scrambled text ends up in an AI training pipeline, does the resulting model get measurably worse? If yes, the scrambling is more than camouflage — it's a payload that damages anything trained on it. If no, ShieldFont is just a hopeful gesture: training pipelines absorb the bad data without consequence.

WHY BOTH MATTER: If H1 is true but H2 is false, ShieldFont protects but doesn't push back. If H2 is true but H1 is false, scrapers extract your content easily but suffer for it later. Best case: both true, in which case ShieldFont is both shield and trap.

3. How AI reads text

To make sense of later sections, here's a no-jargon mental model. An AI language model is essentially a very fast librarian who's read most of the internet. Given any text with a missing word, the librarian guesses what word should fill the gap based on everything they've ever read.

The librarian gets very good at this for normal English because the patterns are everywhere — "the cat sat on the ___" almost always ends with "mat" or "floor." But if you train this librarian on scrambled text — text where "the" has been replaced with "plumb" — they learn weird patterns. "Plumb cat sat on plumb ___." Now what should fill the gap? The librarian's confidence collapses. That collapse is what we measure.

Two technical terms you'll see in charts:

  • Probe accuracy — we ask the model multiple-choice questions like "a plumb-line is used to determine if something is (a) vertical (b) horizontal (c) round." Higher accuracy = the model still knows what "plumb" means.
  • Perplexity — how surprised the model is by the text. Lower is better; around 10-30 is normal English; over 100 means the model finds the text genuinely confusing.

4. An exploratory study, in three rounds

We ran the investigation in three rounds, each informed by the last:

  • Round 1 (the original V2 study): We took the existing ShieldFont mapping (call it M0) and tested whether four frontier AI models could comprehend ShieldFont-encoded articles. This addresses H1.
  • Round 2 (V3): We invented four alternative mapping strategies (M1, M2, M3, M4) and trained a small AI model on encoded text from each, measuring how much the resulting AI was damaged. This addresses H2 broadly.
  • Round 3 (V3.1): Round 2's results pointed at two complementary damage mechanisms; round 3 designed hybrid mappings (M5a, M5b, M6, M6s, M7, M8) that combine them. Re-tested H1 across all 11 mappings to populate the final summary table.

This report presents all three rounds in chronological order, then synthesizes them in the Discussion. The Final Summary Table at the end is the practical "which mapping should I use?" answer.

5. What we measured and how

The model under test

For training-damage tests we used Qwen 2.5 3B (a small but modern open-source AI), fine-tuned with LoRA — a lightweight technique that adjusts the model without retraining from scratch. For the frontier-model comprehension test, we used GPT-5.4, Claude Opus 4.7, Gemini 3.1 Pro, and DeepSeek V3.2 via OpenRouter.

The training mix

For each mapping, we built a training corpus that's mostly clean Wikipedia text with a small fraction of ShieldFont-encoded text mixed in. We tested three contamination levels (1%, 10%, 50%) for the broad sweep, then settled on 10% as the realistic comparison point for the hybrid round. The model trained for 500 iterations on each mix.

How we measured damage

After training, we ran the resulting model through five tests:

  • MMLU — a 57-subject general-knowledge exam (200 questions sampled).
  • HellaSwag — a common-sense reasoning test (200 questions sampled).
  • Substitute-word probes — multiple-choice questions about words the mapping actually swapped, testing whether the model still knows their meanings.
  • Control-word probes — same kind of questions but for words the mapping didn't touch. Difference between substitute and control accuracy reveals whether damage is concentrated on swapped words or general.
  • Perplexity on clean text — how confused the model is by normal Wikipedia after training. Higher = more general damage.
WHY 'SAME LIBRARIAN, DIFFERENT TESTS': Each test isolates a different kind of damage. MMLU drops if the model lost general knowledge. Probes drop if it lost specific words. Perplexity rises if it lost fluency. Together they paint a picture of where the damage went.

Both directions of the size-preference are explained by where the antonyms sit in the mapping construction algorithm. We'll formalize this in the next section.

Focal damage

Substitute words are specifically corrupted; the model's general capability is intact. MMLU and HellaSwag survive, but probes on the swapped words fail. Mechanism: rare-word substitutes (M0's targets, the M5a/M6 fallback words) have weak prior representations the model easily overwrites. Damage is targeted and localized.

Diffuse damage

General capability degrades broadly; substitute and control probes drop together. Mechanism: antonym-encoded text is grammatically natural, so the model partially internalizes the inverted associations. Misinformation spreads across the model's representations, not just the substituted words.

Strategy variants — A1 / A2 / A3 / B / C

  • A1 — antonym-maximal. When a word has both an antonym option and a rare-sub option, antonym wins. Most aggressive antonym usage.
  • A2 — antonym-additive. Words that have a rare-sub option keep it; antonyms are added only for words M0 didn't already cover. Conservative.
  • A3 — GPT-validated overlap. For each overlap word, a GPT model decides whether the antonym partner produces a clean inversion in 3 sample contexts.
  • B — symmetric M0. M0 made bidirectional but no antonyms added. Solves the writer escape-char problem (next section) without changing the damage profile.
  • C — pure antonym. No rare-sub fallback. Sentences without antonym vocabulary leak through verbatim.

10. Discussion: bidirectionality

This is an engineering constraint that doesn't show up in any of our damage metrics, but matters enormously for production deployment.

The current ShieldFont mapping (M0) is asymmetric: "the" is replaced with "plumb" in the HTML source, but not the other way around. The font then renders "plumb" visually as "the." That works fine for normal writers — they type "the" and see "the."

But what if a writer wants to type "plumb" literally? A plumber writing a blog post about plumbing hits this problem immediately. The encoder leaves "plumb" alone (since it's not in the source list), but the font's ligature still renders "plumb" as "the." The plumber's article shows up as gibberish.

Every V3 and V3.1 mapping (M1 through M8) is bidirectional: both members of each pair are in the mapping. So when a writer types "plumb," the encoder swaps it to "the," the font swaps that back to "plumb" visually, and the writer's intent is preserved. The HTML source looks slightly different from what the writer typed, but the rendered page is correct.

BOTTOM LINE: The current ShieldFont mapping (M0) is the only mapping in this study that doesn't solve the writer escape-char problem. Switching to any V3.1 hybrid fixes this AND improves both H1 and H2 scores.

11. Surviving the data pipeline

So far we've measured two things separately: can frontier AI models read encoded text (H1, Studies 1), and does training on encoded text damage models (H2, Studies 2-3)? Both are real threat models — but neither captures what happens to scrambled HTML when a real web-scale data pipeline ingests it.

Industry data pipelines (CommonCrawl post-processing, RefinedWeb, Dolma, CCNet) don't just take everything they crawl. They run filters: language detection, lexical-quality classifiers, and perplexity scoring with a small language model. Documents that look low-quality get dropped before any training begins. So the right question is: does ShieldFont survive these filters and reach a training set at all?

WHY THIS REFRAMES EVERYTHING: A mapping that fools a frontier model into refusing to read your content is excellent for direct protection, but if a perplexity filter drops it from the training set, ShieldFont becomes pure camouflage — never reaching the model at training time. Conversely, a mapping whose encoded text passes every filter and reaches training is a true poisoning payload — but if it also reads as natural English, then crawlers extract most of the original content easily. These two goals (protection now / poisoning later) turn out to be fundamentally in tension.

Three filter stages tested

  1. Crawler extraction — built sample HTML pages, served via local HTTP, scraped with three widely-used extractors (requests+BeautifulSoup, trafilatura, readability-lxml). Measured % of original article text that leaked through.
  2. Language ID + lexical quality — ran each encoded article through langdetect (Google CLD port) and a wordfreq top-50k dictionary check. These are cheap pre-filters every pipeline runs.
  3. GPT-2 perplexity filter — RefinedWeb-style filter using openai-community/gpt2 (124M). Documents above PPL threshold get dropped.

13. Limitations

  1. One model size only. All training-damage results are on Qwen 2.5 3B. Larger models may behave differently.
  2. LoRA, not full fine-tuning. A real attacker training on web-scale data with full fine-tuning may see different effects.
  3. Short training runs. 500 iterations is brief. Real production training runs are 10-1000× longer.
  4. 200-question subsets. MMLU and HellaSwag have ~3.5pp standard error per condition at this size. Effects below 5pp are suggestive rather than definitive.
  5. Probe quality varies. Probes were templated by GPT-4.1-mini and only spot-checked. Some may have soft distractors that undertest the model.
  6. Antonym set is small (308 pairs max). GPT-4.1 hit a wall validating new antonym pairs beyond WordNet's 199. Hand curation could expand this.
  7. One contamination pattern. Real scrapes mix protected and unprotected pages in messier ways than our clean 1%/10%/50% mixes.

14. Recommendations for follow-up studies

  1. Replicate at 7B-8B model scale. All findings here are on Qwen 2.5 3B. The asymmetry between common-word robustness and rare-word fragility may scale with model capacity. Why this matters: real production training uses much larger models.
  2. Full fine-tuning, not LoRA. Real attackers don't use LoRA at scale. M1/M3/M4's null result may not hold under stronger training. Why this matters: a stronger threat model could change the recommendations.
  3. Build a high-quality 500-pair antonym set by hand. GPT-4.1 hit a wall at ~108 new validated pairs. Hand curation would test the size axis properly. Why this matters: M5b's underperformance vs M5a may be specific to weak validation, not a general size effect.
  4. Test action-pair and modal antonyms (give/take, must/may, certain/uncertain). We explicitly excluded these categories. They may behave differently from temporal/spatial/quantifier antonyms. Why this matters: more categories = more potential antonym coverage = potentially better hybrid mappings.
  5. Cross-tokenizer test. Rebuild encoded data using Llama or Mistral tokenizers; train and evaluate. Why this matters: if results differ across tokenizers, the V3 findings are tokenizer-specific.
  6. Real-world web-crawl simulation. Mix real Common Crawl with N% ShieldFont-encoded pages drawn from a deployed site, train, measure. Why this matters: 1%/10%/50% controlled mixes are clean but artificial; real scrapes have messier statistics.
  7. Long-horizon training. 500 iterations is brief. Effects may compound (or wash out) over 5,000+ iterations as in production training runs. Why this matters: poisoning effects may be different at scale.
  8. Per-domain protection analysis. Coverage rates differ by content type — news, scientific text, dialogue. Measure each. Why this matters: a mapping that covers Wikipedia well may leak on Reddit.
