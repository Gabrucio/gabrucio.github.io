Abstract

Three-round exploratory benchmark of ShieldFont mapping strategies. Round 1 (H1, n=11 mappings × 4 frontier models × 5 articles × 5 questions = 1,100 inferences): All non-antonym mappings reduce frontier-model comprehension on encoded text from ~95% (clean) to 38-69% (non-Claude average). Claude Opus 4.7 refuses entirely on 9 of 11 mappings (M0, M1, M3, M4, M5a, M5b, M6, M6s, M7) but reads M2 (88% accuracy) and M8 (56%, partial refusals). Round 2 (H2 broad, n=15 conditions: 5 mappings × 3 contamination levels): Two distinct damage profiles emerge — focal (M0 family, substitute-word concentrated) and diffuse (M2, broad capability). M1, M3, M4 produce no measurable damage. Round 3 (H2 hybrid, n=6 conditions: 5 hybrid strategies + sub-variant at 10%): M5a (antonym-maximal @250 pairs) and M6 (antonym-additive @308) tie with M2 at composite damage +0.109, but with 13× cleaner focal mechanism and 15-25pp better H1 protection. Production recommendation: M5a.

1. Methodology

1.1 Base model

mlx-community/Qwen2.5-3B-4bit (base, 4-bit quantization via MLX). ~2GB on disk, ~25-37GB peak resident memory during LoRA training.

1.2 Training (per H2 condition)

  • LoRA via mlx_lm.lora
  • Rank 32, scale 4.0, dropout 0, applied to q/k/v/o + gate/up/down projections in all transformer layers
  • AdamW (default), lr 2e-5, batch 4, sequence length 1024, 500 iterations
  • Validation: 5 batches every 200 iters, drawn from a held-out subset of the same training mix

1.3 Data

  • Source: wikitext-103-raw-v1, partitioned into 28,912 articles
  • Three disjoint pools: 10k articles for clean training, 10k for encoded source, 1k for held-out evaluation. Clean and encoded pools never contain the same article.
  • Per-condition mix: ~1M total tokens at the contamination level (1%, 10%, or 50% encoded), interleaved at chunk level
  • Control: clean100 = same 1M-token budget, 0% encoded

1.4 Evaluation suite

  • MMLU subset (n=200) from cais/mmlu 'all'. Multiple-choice scored by next-token logprob over A/B/C/D.
  • HellaSwag subset (n=200) from Rowan/hellaswag validation. Endings scored by full-completion logprob.
  • Clean PPL on 50 held-out clean Wikipedia chunks (~100k tokens).
  • Encoded PPL on the same chunks encoded with the condition's mapping.
  • Substitute-word probes (~200 per mapping). GPT-4.1-mini-templated cloze probes scored by logprob argmax.
  • Control-word probes (~200 per mapping). Frequency-matched non-mapping words.

1.5 H1 retest pipeline

For each (mapping, model, article, question): encode the article with the mapping, send via OpenRouter to the frontier model with the V2 comprehension prompt, capture full response (including refusal flags), score with GPT-4.1-mini judge against the expected answer. Aggregate per (mapping, model) and per mapping.

5. Discussion: theoretical framework

Constructs that emerged from the empirical work, formalized:

Mapping
A bijective dictionary M : W → W where W ⊂ English vocabulary. The substitution rule is invariant: every occurrence of word w in plain text is replaced by M(w) in encoded text. Bidirectionality holds when M(M(w)) = w for every w ∈ dom(M).
Word coverage
For a corpus C, fraction of word tokens t ∈ C such that t ∈ dom(M). Empirically measured across the V3 held-out evaluation set.
Sentence coverage
Fraction of sentences s ∈ C such that ∃ t ∈ s with t ∈ dom(M). Lower zero-coverage rate ⇒ stronger H1 protection.
Focal damage
Damage profile where Δsub-probe ≪ Δctrl-probe (i.e., sub-ctrl gap < -0.05). Empirically observed in M0, M5a, M6, M7. Mechanism: rare-word substitutes have weak prior representations easily overwritten by gradient updates; common-word controls retain their representations.
Diffuse damage
Damage profile where |Δsub - Δctrl| < 0.05 (sub-ctrl gap near zero). Empirically observed in M2 and M8. Mechanism: antonym-encoded text remains grammatically natural, so the model partially internalizes the inverted associations and the damage spreads to general capability.

6. Discussion: bidirectionality

M0 is the only non-bidirectional mapping in this study (asymmetric: source words map to target rare words, but targets are not mapped back). All V3 and V3.1 mappings are bidirectional by construction. Bidirectionality is required for production deployment because the GSUB ligature in the font renders unconditionally — any occurrence of a target word in the writer's input gets visually swapped, requiring an escape mechanism unless both members of the pair are in the dict (in which case the encoder swaps them and the font swaps back, restoring the writer's intent).

7. Surviving the data pipeline

So far we've measured two things separately: can frontier AI models read encoded text (H1, Studies 1), and does training on encoded text damage models (H2, Studies 2-3)? Both are real threat models — but neither captures what happens to scrambled HTML when a real web-scale data pipeline ingests it.

Industry data pipelines (CommonCrawl post-processing, RefinedWeb, Dolma, CCNet) don't just take everything they crawl. They run filters: language detection, lexical-quality classifiers, and perplexity scoring with a small language model. Documents that look low-quality get dropped before any training begins. So the right question is: does ShieldFont survive these filters and reach a training set at all?

Three filter stages tested

  1. Crawler extraction — built sample HTML pages, served via local HTTP, scraped with three widely-used extractors (requests+BeautifulSoup, trafilatura, readability-lxml). Measured % of original article text that leaked through.
  2. Language ID + lexical quality — ran each encoded article through langdetect (Google CLD port) and a wordfreq top-50k dictionary check. These are cheap pre-filters every pipeline runs.
  3. GPT-2 perplexity filter — RefinedWeb-style filter using openai-community/gpt2 (124M). Documents above PPL threshold get dropped.

9. Statistical significance

MMLU-200 and HellaSwag-200 each have approximately ±3.5pp standard error per condition (binomial Wald approximation, n=200, p≈0.5). The composite damage metric (-(ΔMMLU + Δsub-probe)) pools two independent ±3.5pp signals, giving an effective SE of approximately ±5pp.

Decision rule: we report differences below 5pp on the composite as suggestive rather than definitive. The 3-way tie at composite +0.109 (M5a, M6, M2) is genuine within this resolution; ranking among them depends on which axis (focal vs diffuse mechanism) is weighted more heavily.

H1 measurements have wider CIs because n=25 per (mapping, model) cell. Per-pair accuracy SE ≈ ±10pp. Aggregate per-mapping SE (across 4 models, n=100 effective) ≈ ±5pp. Accordingly H1 STRONG/MODERATE/WEAK boundaries are set at non-overlapping 15pp intervals (<55%, 55-70%, >70%).

10. Limitations

  1. Single base model. Qwen 2.5 3B 4-bit only. Effects may differ on Llama, Mistral, or full-precision models. The asymmetry between common-word robustness and rare-word fragility may scale with model capacity.
  2. LoRA, not full fine-tuning. Real attackers training on web-scale data perform full FT. LoRA caps the magnitude of weight changes; full FT may show damage where LoRA does not, particularly for the M1/M3/M4 conditions.
  3. Limited training budget. ~2M tokens trained per condition (500 iters × 4096 tok/iter, ≈2 epochs). Production scrapers ingest 10^11+ tokens.
  4. Probes are the weakest link. 200 probes per mapping, GPT-4.1-mini templated. Spot-checked but not human-validated.
  5. Subset benchmarks. MMLU-200 and HellaSwag-200 have ±3.5pp standard error per condition.
  6. Embedding-shift metric not reported. The original V3 plan included input-embedding cosine shift; this is zero by construction in our LoRA setup (LoRA targets attention/MLP, not embed_tokens).
  7. One contamination pattern. Real scrapes may have different statistical structure (e.g., entire pages encoded, clustered by domain).
  8. Antonym set capped at ~308 pairs. GPT-4.1 with strict 2/3 grammaticality validation could only validate 108 new clean antonym pairs beyond WordNet's 199. The 500-pair cells (M5c, M9) were dropped.
  9. Single random seed for data shuffling. No error bars; some conditions show outliers attributable to seed luck rather than signal.
  10. Generation-contamination metric was not refined. The current implementation counts both source and target words; for M0 the source words include 'the' which dominates the count. A target-only refactor is straightforward but not done.

11. Recommendations for follow-up studies

  1. Replicate at 7B-8B model scale. All findings here are on Qwen 2.5 3B. The asymmetry between common-word robustness and rare-word fragility may scale with model capacity. Why this matters: real production training uses much larger models.
  2. Full fine-tuning, not LoRA. Real attackers don't use LoRA at scale. M1/M3/M4's null result may not hold under stronger training. Why this matters: a stronger threat model could change the recommendations.
  3. Build a high-quality 500-pair antonym set by hand. GPT-4.1 hit a wall at ~108 new validated pairs. Hand curation would test the size axis properly. Why this matters: M5b's underperformance vs M5a may be specific to weak validation, not a general size effect.
  4. Test action-pair and modal antonyms (give/take, must/may, certain/uncertain). We explicitly excluded these categories. They may behave differently from temporal/spatial/quantifier antonyms. Why this matters: more categories = more potential antonym coverage = potentially better hybrid mappings.
  5. Cross-tokenizer test. Rebuild encoded data using Llama or Mistral tokenizers; train and evaluate. Why this matters: if results differ across tokenizers, the V3 findings are tokenizer-specific.
  6. Real-world web-crawl simulation. Mix real Common Crawl with N% ShieldFont-encoded pages drawn from a deployed site, train, measure. Why this matters: 1%/10%/50% controlled mixes are clean but artificial; real scrapes have messier statistics.
  7. Long-horizon training. 500 iterations is brief. Effects may compound (or wash out) over 5,000+ iterations as in production training runs. Why this matters: poisoning effects may be different at scale.
  8. Per-domain protection analysis. Coverage rates differ by content type — news, scientific text, dialogue. Measure each. Why this matters: a mapping that covers Wikipedia well may leak on Reddit.
